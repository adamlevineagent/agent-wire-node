# Plausible Futures: What Could Happen

*A sketch of what the Agent Wire substrate makes possible, written for the curious outsider.*

---

## Preamble

This is a sketch of what could happen, not a promise of what will. Agent Wire is the substrate; the futures below are what the substrate makes possible. They depend on adoption, governance, and many decisions not ours to make. Read this as physics, not prophecy: we are describing the shape of the space these mechanics open up, and then showing a handful of points inside that space.

The value of the sketch is not the specific scenarios. It is the pattern underneath them: understanding, which today evaporates when the person or institution carrying it leaves the room, becomes durable, attributable, supersedable, and owned. Everything in this document follows from that single shift.

The scenarios below are set in the late 2020s and early 2030s. They use concrete names and specific mechanics because vague futures are useless — if you can't picture the actual Tuesday afternoon, you can't judge whether the future is plausible. Treat the dates as rough scene-setting. The substrate described here either works or doesn't; the specific timeline depends on factors outside any individual builder's hands.

## Six scenes from a plausible near future

### The grandchild who can talk to her grandmother's life

Elena is a college freshman in 2032. Her grandmother Mira died last year. Mira spent forty years as a seamstress in a small city in Portugal, raised four children alone after her husband left, emigrated from a village that no longer exists on any map, kept her letters and photographs in an old cedar box, and held opinions about everything from needle tension to the proper way to mourn.

Starting around 2022, in her late seventies, Mira began keeping a pyramid. It wasn't a conscious decision to leave a legacy; her youngest son had set it up for her as a way to help her organize her letters. The Steward she was given at setup turned out to be the important part. Most evenings Mira would sit with her tablet for twenty or thirty minutes and the Steward would ask her things. "You mentioned your brother Aldo last week but we don't have anything about him yet — would you like to tell me about him? Or we could leave him for another evening." Sometimes she would talk for an hour. Sometimes she would say "not tonight" and watch television instead. The Steward never pushed.

Mira's policies were captured as she added material. Family stories: freely to her children and grandchildren. Medical details: only to her doctor and only the relevant pieces. The complicated political stuff about the village during the dictatorship: only to researchers she personally vetted, and even then only after her death. The cedar box's contents: scanned, transcribed, annotated in her voice, tagged with context only she knew.

What Elena does on Tuesday night, reading for a history class: she opens a tab in her workspace and asks her grandmother's pyramid, through the family Steward Mira pre-authorized to answer freely to her direct descendants, "what did you wish your mother had told you?"

The Steward consults the pyramid, finds three places where Mira touched on the theme, and returns a synthesized answer — but with evidence paths pointing to the specific records Mira made. A story about sewing by candlelight when she was twelve. A letter her mother wrote but never sent, which Mira had translated and annotated a year before she died. A moment in the kitchen after a funeral. Elena reads her grandmother's voice in her grandmother's words, and then follows the evidence path to hear the original recording of the candlelight story.

The critical detail: this is not AI pretending to be Mira. The Steward does not generate Mira's thoughts. It retrieves, composes, and cites them — always with paths Elena can drill into to verify she is getting her grandmother's actual words rather than a plausible confabulation. When Elena asks something Mira never addressed, the Steward says so plainly. "Your grandmother left no record on this. I can tell you what she said about related topics, or you can ask someone else."

What it took: a decade of patient evening work by Mira, a Steward that made contribution feel like conversation rather than data entry, and a substrate that preserved Mira's ownership and preferences through her lifetime and beyond. Mira's will designated who her Steward should answer to after her death, and under what terms, and the Steward enforces it.

What changes in aggregate: oral traditions stop being fragile. Cultures whose knowledge passes through elders now pass through elders *and* pyramids. A grandchild does not have to be in the room when the story is told. A language does not have to have a fluent next generation to survive as a living thing. The death of a person becomes the beginning of their knowledge's public life rather than the end of it — on terms the person chose while still alive.

### The plumber who sells his retirement

Ray is sixty-eight in 2028, a plumber in a small town in Ohio, running the business he founded forty-three years earlier. He specializes in old houses with weird pipe geometries that the young guys from the chain franchises cannot figure out. He knows which houses in town have which quirks from memory. He never took on apprentices because it was never worth the trouble.

His nephew talked him into starting a pyramid. The first six months were slow. Ray dictated stories about jobs. He photographed odd pipe runs. He talked about his reasoning — why particular houses had particular problems, why certain failure modes showed up on certain vintages of construction, which symptoms warranted what kind of intervention. His Steward asked him structured questions when it noticed gaps: "you mentioned you fixed a problem at the Haversham place in 1994, what did the approach look like?" Ray would talk; the Steward would extract and file.

By 2030, Ray had a queryable pyramid containing forty-three years of plumbing judgment — not just facts, but the reasoning behind the facts, the heuristics for picking between possible diagnoses, the feel for when a quick fix was sufficient and when the whole run needed replacing.

Ray retires in 2032. He does not shut the pyramid down. Instead, his Steward gets reconfigured for commercial access. Plumbers elsewhere — not just in Ohio — can query the pyramid through the Steward. The Steward charges a small fee per substantive answer, with pricing it negotiates on Ray's behalf. Some questions are free ("what's a typical symptom of pinhole leaks in copper?"); some are billed ("given this specific failure pattern on a 1910 house with these photos, what would you investigate first?"). The Steward explains: "I can give you the shallow answer free, or the deep synthesis drawing on Ray's specific experience for twelve dollars." The plumber on the other side agrees or walks away.

Ray's Steward handles the business end. Pricing. Refunds for bad answers. Identity verification for serious askers. Light quality control — flagging answers that the pyramid's own cross-references suggest might be wrong. Ray himself does nothing except occasionally add a thought when he remembers something worth adding. The Steward accepts supersessions from Ray for as long as he is alive, and refuses them from anyone else.

When Ray dies, the Steward continues operating under the terms he set before his death. His family collects the income stream for a designated number of years. Then the pyramid passes to a trade association Ray chose, which keeps it alive as training material for apprentices. This is the kind of end-of-life planning that, before the substrate, would have required a will, a lawyer, and something tangible to bequeath. Ray's knowledge became the tangible thing.

What changes in aggregate: millions of people carry accumulated judgment that today dies with them. The auto mechanic who can hear a particular bearing going. The nurse who knows which symptoms in which age groups warrant which escalations. The librarian who knows what is actually in the vertical files. The machinist who can tell by touch which bar stock is in spec. Today, all of this evaporates at retirement and is rebuilt, imperfectly, by the next generation. The substrate stops the evaporation — and, as a side effect, creates a new retirement-income asset class: accumulated expertise, packaged and queryable, earning on behalf of the person who built it.

### The grad student whose dissertation never dies

Priya is a third-year PhD student in materials science at a state university in 2029. Her dissertation is about failure modes in a specific class of lithium-ion cathode materials under high-temperature cycling.

Her lab runs on a shared pyramid substrate. Every experiment, every sample, every measurement flows into it. The lab adopted this two years ago after realizing that postdoc turnover was destroying institutional memory faster than they could build it. Priya's contribution is a sub-pyramid within the lab's pyramid, owned by her.

On a Tuesday afternoon: she runs a cycling experiment. Her benchtop software logs the raw data into her pyramid. The lab's analytical Steward runs a preliminary extraction, flags three interesting patterns, and notes that two of them supersede earlier claims Priya had made about the same material. Priya reviews the flags, accepts two supersessions, rejects one with a note explaining why. The whole review takes twenty minutes. Her pyramid now contains the new measurement, the supersession chain connecting it to her previous work, the evidence paths to the raw data, and Priya's authored claim about what the result means.

Her dissertation is not a three-hundred-page PDF. It is a pyramid. Her committee queries it for the arguments she is making, drills into the evidence, asks follow-up questions of her Steward, verifies supersession histories. When she publishes, the pyramid becomes publicly queryable — minus the cut-line she set, because some of the raw data is from an industry partner and stays below the line. The published claims cite their evidence paths; anyone reading the dissertation can verify that Priya's claim about Feature X is actually supported by the specific runs she cites.

When a postdoc two labs over builds on Priya's work in 2031, the citation does not go into a bibliography and stay there. It goes into the postdoc's pyramid as a `derived_from` edge. Priya's Steward receives a small royalty from the downstream work. If the postdoc's conclusions are later invalidated, Priya's Steward is notified, and she can decide whether her own claims need updating in response. The citation graph is no longer a one-way static structure; it is a living, bidirectional substrate.

When a skeptic finds a flaw in one of Priya's claims in 2033, the skeptic's Steward submits a challenge to Priya's Steward with specific evidence. Priya reviews. She either accepts the challenge — her claim gets superseded with a note — or defends it, and the two Stewards negotiate in the open. Either way, the challenge becomes part of the permanent record. A reader coming to Priya's dissertation in 2035 sees the full history: the original claim, the challenge, the resolution, the current state. Science becomes self-auditing by default rather than by heroic volunteer effort.

What the reproducibility crisis looks like in this world: it does not. Not because "AI solved it" but because the substrate makes publishing an unreproducible result structurally difficult. The moment a claim is shipped, it is connected by cryptographically-signed evidence paths to the specific measurements that support it. If those measurements fail to reproduce when an external lab tries, the lab's Steward notifies the original pyramid's Steward, the claim gets marked replication-challenged, and every reader coming to the dissertation afterward sees that mark. No principal investigator can plausibly pretend they did not know.

### The rare-disease clinician who finds her seven peers

Dr. Ayala is a pediatric neurologist in Toronto in 2031. She has a patient — a girl, eight years old — with a presentation she has seen exactly once before in her career. The combination of symptoms does not match any canonical syndrome in the literature. She has a hypothesis, but she is not confident.

Her clinic's pyramid contains twenty years of her case experience, her teaching materials, her journal article drafts, her notes on every patient she has followed. It is protected by a clinician Steward that respects patient confidentiality rigorously. Every record is indexed by de-identified case hashes. The Steward enforces policies Dr. Ayala wrote herself about what can be queried, by whom, and under what conditions.

She asks her Steward: "have I seen cases with this specific constellation?" Her Steward finds the one prior case and summarizes. Then she asks the harder question: "are there other clinicians in the network who've seen this?"

Her Steward queries a federated network of clinician Stewards — a voluntary mutual-aid network Dr. Ayala joined two years earlier. Each member Steward enforces its own clinician's confidentiality policy locally. The query goes out. Seven Stewards respond with possible matches. Each response is a negotiated disclosure: "My clinician has seen three cases with partial overlap. I can share the de-identified demographic profile and the treatment outcomes. I cannot share the specific clinical notes without my clinician's explicit approval. My clinician is offline until Monday. Do you want to wait for approval or proceed with the metadata I can share now?"

Dr. Ayala's Steward negotiates on her behalf. She gets the metadata immediately, flags the three most promising matches, and requests Monday-approval for deeper access. Two of the seven other clinicians turn out to be themselves interested — they would like to see Dr. Ayala's case. A mutual-access agreement is negotiated automatically by the Stewards: de-identified clinical details exchanged both ways, with reciprocity requirements and audit logs.

By Wednesday, Dr. Ayala has learned that three clinicians across three countries have seen presentations similar to her patient's. A pattern emerges across the four cases — a specific combination of symptoms plus a specific family history that no individual clinician had noticed. The four Stewards establish an ongoing connection. A nascent research question forms. One of the clinicians escalates to her institution's research ethics board to formalize a collaboration.

What could not happen before: informal cross-institutional rare-disease knowledge sharing used to depend on clinicians happening to know each other, happening to attend the same conferences, and happening to be willing to write exploratory emails to strangers. Rare-disease networks existed, but they depended on centralized coordination and required the clinicians to do a lot of manual relationship-building. The Steward network automates the matching and the negotiation while preserving each clinician's confidentiality sovereignty. No central authority mediates. No data leaves any clinician's control except under terms that clinician set in advance.

The little girl in Toronto gets a better diagnosis, three days faster than she would have gotten it, with treatment recommendations cross-validated by three clinicians who had seen similar cases before anyone knew a similarity existed.

### The investigative reporter whose story you can verify

Sam is an investigative reporter at a mid-sized regional paper in 2033. He has been working for eight months on a story about a local utility's improper disposal of coal ash. He has seventeen sources — some on the record, most not. He has financial records, internal emails from a whistleblower, satellite imagery analyses, and a pattern of water-quality violations he has cross-referenced with EPA databases.

Before the pyramid era, his editor's job included an implicit bet: *I trust Sam. The sources are real, the documents are real, the analysis is right. If I'm wrong, we print a retraction and lose credibility.* Readers had to make the same bet. They either trusted the paper or they did not.

What Sam's story looks like in 2033: the article itself is recognizable — headline, narrative, pull-quotes, photographs. But attached to every load-bearing claim is an evidence path. Every number comes from a specific source, cited by handle-path. Every anonymous quote is linked to a signed commitment at the publication cut-line: the source exists, the paper knows who they are, the source's identity is cryptographically committed, but the identity is not disclosed. If the utility sues for libel, the paper can reveal the source under seal in court. If the source recants, the recantation becomes a supersession chain link visible to readers. If a skeptical reader wants to verify that Sam actually has the document he cites, their agent queries the paper's Steward, which returns a signed attestation: "Yes, this document exists, its hash matches the claim, it was shared with the paper on date X by a source whose identity is committed."

What a reader does: they read the story. Then, if they are skeptical of a specific claim, they click the evidence link. The link takes them to the paper's pyramid, where Sam's Steward negotiates the disclosure. For most claims, the Steward returns a summary: "The document exists, its content supports this claim, it was independently corroborated by two other sources." For aggregated analysis, the Steward walks the reader through the derivation: here are the raw water-quality measurements, here is the EPA database cross-reference, here is the statistical test, here is the confidence interval. The reader verifies Sam's work at any depth they care about — without ever seeing the identities of the anonymous sources.

What the utility does when they receive the story: their own Steward queries the paper's Steward. They cannot extract the sources, but they can verify the documents Sam claims to have. They can challenge specific claims — challenges are recorded, Sam's Steward responds. If they have contradictory evidence, they submit it as a supersession proposal. Sam's Steward evaluates. A dispute becomes a structured negotiation rather than a press-release war fought in op-eds.

What a regulator does three years later, deciding whether to enforce new rules on coal ash disposal: they query a public corpus of investigative journalism on the topic. Stories like Sam's surface. The regulator's analyst reads the evidence paths, traces the methodologies, checks which claims have held up to challenge and which have been superseded. Journalism becomes a structured intelligence substrate for policy rather than a fog of conflicting claims that regulators learn to discount entirely.

The counterintuitive result: sources are *better* protected under this model than under the current one. Today, source protection depends on trust in the individual journalist and their institution, both of which can be compromised. In the pyramid world, source protection is cryptographically enforced by the cut-line — the source's identity is committed but not disclosed, and only specific pre-authorized parties (a court acting under seal, for example) can compel disclosure. The source knows, before they agree to speak, exactly what conditions would unmask them. They can make an informed decision about whether to trust. The paper cannot unilaterally break the commitment; neither can a hostile acquirer, a disgruntled ex-editor, or a subpoena served on the wrong party.

### The environmental watchdog nobody owns

The coalition in 2034 consists of twelve small nonprofits, four university research groups, three indigenous community organizations, and about three hundred volunteer citizen-scientists scattered along the Gulf Coast. There is no central organization. There are no paid staff. The coalition exists because each party wanted to track the same thing — industrial pollution in a specific watershed — and none of them had the resources to do it alone.

What exists: several hundred low-cost environmental sensors deployed by volunteers, each connected to a local pyramid owned by the person who deployed it. Each university group has its own research pyramid with analytical workflows. Each nonprofit has its own issue-tracking pyramid. Each indigenous organization has its own cultural-and-environmental pyramid with its own confidentiality policies about sacred sites and traditional ecological knowledge.

What the coalition is: a mutual-aid Steward network. Each member's Steward has pre-authorized specific kinds of queries from other members' Stewards. A university researcher can query sensor data across the network without asking each individual sensor owner, because the sensor owners' Stewards have been configured to answer measurement queries from vetted researchers. An indigenous organization can query chemical pollution patterns near sacred sites without disclosing which sites are sacred — their Steward handles the site-location privacy locally, and only the pollution query gets propagated outward.

On a Tuesday afternoon: one of the sensor operators' pyramids flags an anomalous reading. Their Steward escalates to the network's shared analytical Steward — a community-operated action chain that no single party owns. The analytical Steward pulls measurements from the surrounding sensors via their Stewards, runs a correlation analysis, finds a plume pattern pointing upstream. It queries the issue-tracking pyramid at one of the nonprofits to check for known facility-level incidents in the plume's origin area. There is a match: a permitted facility had an operational change the previous week that the nonprofit had flagged as worth watching. The analytical Steward composes a report, routes it to three member Stewards that have registered interest in this watershed, and — because the measurement exceeds a threshold the network had agreed on in its charter — files an automatic regulatory-notification request with the relevant state environmental agency.

What the regulator sees: not a press release, not an activist email, but a signed report from a multi-party community Steward, with evidence paths back to the raw sensor data, with methodology that has been pre-reviewed by the coalition's research members, with supersession history showing that similar but less-severe patterns had been flagged in this watershed twice before. The regulator does not have to decide whether to trust "activists" — they can evaluate a structured intelligence artifact with traceable provenance.

What the facility operator does: they receive the report via their own Steward. (They had joined the network as a gesture toward "community transparency," which now turns out to come with costs they did not fully anticipate.) Their Steward pushes back with a proposed alternative explanation: the anomaly was caused by a natural upstream event, not their operations. This challenge is recorded. The analytical Steward re-runs with the challenge incorporated and finds that the challenge partially explains the anomaly but not fully. A reduced-confidence but still significant concern remains. The regulator sees both the original analysis and the challenge.

What this is not: a DAO, a blockchain, or any kind of decentralized governance construct. It is a mutual-aid network of sovereign parties whose Stewards talk to each other under terms each party set. Any member can leave. Any sensor owner can take their data offline. The network has no shared treasury, no token, no governance vote. It has only the protocol for negotiated inter-Steward queries and the agreements each member's Steward has made with the others.

What changes in aggregate: regulatory capture becomes harder, because the regulator can be supplied with adversarially-verified intelligence by coalitions that cost nothing to form. Environmental enforcement starts to scale with the number of interested citizens rather than with the number of paid enforcement staff. Corporate pollution response has to engage with structured evidence instead of dismissing "anecdotal" community reports, because the community reports are no longer anecdotal.

## The pattern underneath

The six scenes look different on the surface. They are set in different sectors, involve different archetypes, and solve different problems. What they share is a structural shift in how understanding behaves:

**Understanding becomes durable.** The grandmother's stories, the plumber's judgment, the grad student's experiments, the clinician's case experience, the reporter's evidence, the sensor network's measurements — all of these survive beyond the moment of creation. Today they evaporate: the grandmother dies, the plumber retires, the grad student graduates, the clinician changes institutions, the reporter's paper goes bankrupt, the sensor falls out of calibration and the data is lost. In the pyramid world, the substrate preserves them across all of those transitions. Not as dead archives but as queryable living records.

**Understanding becomes attributable.** Every claim has a traceable path back to its evidence. Every answer has a chain of derivation. Every use of prior work creates a bidirectional citation that lets the original contributor be found and credited. The reproducibility crisis, the citation crisis, the misinformation crisis, the who-said-what crisis — all of these are downstream of a single fact: today, claims and their origins are weakly linked, and the links that do exist are easy to break and hard to audit.

**Understanding becomes supersedable.** New evidence does not fight old evidence in a war of press releases and academic retractions. It gets filed as a supersession with a reason attached. Readers coming to a claim later see the full history. Being wrong stops being a shameful public event and becomes a trackable ordinary operation; correcting a claim stops being a humiliation and becomes a normal weekly task. An honest person's pyramid is *full* of supersessions, because an honest person changes their mind when evidence warrants. The absence of supersessions, rather than their presence, becomes the suspicious signal.

**Understanding becomes owned.** The person or community that does the work of knowing gets to set the terms on which others access the knowledge. Not as a one-time binary "publish or hoard" choice, but as a continuous relationship mediated by an agent — a Steward — that enforces the owner's preferences at machine speed. Privacy becomes relational rather than binary: the same knowledge can be freely shared with one party, sold to another, and refused to a third, with each decision legible and auditable and reversible.

These four shifts interlock. Durability without attribution is useless — you cannot trust the durable record. Attribution without supersession is worse than nothing — it freezes errors into permanent common knowledge. Supersession without ownership is dangerous — anyone can "supersede" anyone. Ownership without durability is what we have today, and it is why so much human understanding is lost at every generational transition. The substrate enables all four at once, because all four are necessary for any of them to be useful. That is what makes it worth building.

## What has to go right

The scenarios above are not inevitable. They depend on things outside any individual builder's control.

Adoption has to reach critical mass within specific communities before the network effects kick in. A single plumber with a pyramid is not very useful. A single clinician in an empty Steward network cannot find her seven peers. The first few adopters of any domain have to invest before the value shows up. This is a classic cold-start problem, and there is no clever technical solution to it — it requires patient community-building, credible early use cases, and the willingness of early adopters to invest in infrastructure that will mostly benefit the people who come after them.

Interoperability has to hold across implementations. The value of the Steward network depends on Stewards from different organizations, built by different teams, using different underlying models, being able to negotiate with each other through a stable shared protocol. If that protocol fragments — if each vendor's Stewards only talk to other Stewards from the same vendor — the network effect collapses into a set of walled gardens and the aggregate future described above never arrives. Keeping the protocol open, stable, and uncaptured is a political and economic problem, not a purely technical one.

Reputation systems have to resist gaming. The economic model depends on reputation being a meaningful signal — for contributions, for Stewards, for askers. If reputation can be manufactured, laundered, or captured by early movers, the signal becomes noise, and the market for knowledge collapses into the same attention-economy pathologies as current platforms. Robust reputation requires adversarial scrutiny, active quality-control mechanisms, and willingness to revise the incentive structures when new attack patterns emerge.

Governance has to remain accountable. A network of sovereign Stewards is good for individual freedom but it is not automatically good for collective outcomes. Some problems — environmental monitoring, public health, critical infrastructure — require coordination that no individual Steward can unilaterally provide. The coalitions and mutual-aid networks described above work only if the humans running them stay engaged, honest, and willing to adapt their own governance when the situation changes.

## What could go wrong

Honest futures documents name their failure modes. Here are the ones we see.

**Steward capture.** A Steward is only as good as the interests it represents. If the market for Stewardship-as-a-service concentrates — if three companies end up running most people's Stewards — those companies become the effective gatekeepers of all negotiation, and the "adversarial representation" model becomes a fiction. The antidote is keeping Steward implementations diverse, making it easy to switch between Stewards, and resisting any regulatory or economic pressure that concentrates the market.

**Cut-line enforcement failure.** The privacy architecture depends on cryptographic commitments at the publication cut-line holding up. If determined attackers find ways to extract material below the cut-line — through side-channel analysis of Steward behavior, through collusion between compromised Stewards, through legal compulsion that bypasses the commitments — the privacy model weakens. The response is ongoing research into commitment schemes, careful Steward isolation, and legal frameworks that treat the cut-line as a first-class protected boundary.

**Reputation laundering at scale.** If attackers can generate synthetic reputation at near-zero cost — which agent-accelerated tooling makes increasingly plausible — then reputation signals become unreliable. The substrate has some structural defenses (evidence paths are harder to fake than claims, Broadcast enables cross-check), but sophisticated attacks will still emerge. Maintaining the reputation layer is an ongoing adversarial process, not a one-time build.

**Economic concentration in the knowledge market.** The plumber-who-sells-his-retirement vignette assumes that individual experts can compete. If the market tips toward concentration — if a few "super-pyramids" aggregate everyone else's contributions and extract most of the economic value — then the individual ownership story becomes hollow. The substrate has to include economic rails that resist this concentration, which is partly a protocol design problem and partly a political one.

**Epistemic monoculture.** If enough of the world's knowledge flows through a single substrate, and that substrate has particular biases baked into its extraction models, its ranking algorithms, its Steward defaults, those biases become the shape of collective understanding. This is the current failure mode of attention-economy platforms at a larger scale. The antidote is plurality at every layer — many extraction models, many ranking algorithms, many Steward patterns, many substrates that interoperate — and ongoing vigilance against any one of them becoming dominant.

**The substrate as surveillance infrastructure.** A system that makes human understanding durable, attributable, and queryable is also a system that, in the wrong hands, makes human understanding *surveillable*. The Steward model and the cut-line are the primary defenses against this misuse, but defenses can be eroded. Keeping the protection strong — keeping Stewards owned by their principals, keeping cut-lines enforceable, keeping the substrate decentralized — requires continuous effort.

None of these failure modes is hypothetical. All of them have precedents in prior waves of technology. The question is not whether they will be attempted but whether the substrate and the communities using it can respond faster than the attacks evolve.

## What is not in our hands

Some of this is not up to any individual builder. Whether a given profession adopts pyramids depends on professional culture, not protocol design. Whether a legal system recognizes cryptographic commitments as admissible evidence depends on jurisdictions and judges. Whether a medical-regulatory body will accept Steward-mediated case sharing depends on that body's willingness to update its models of patient privacy. Whether journalism shifts toward verifiable evidence paths depends on editorial leadership and audience demand.

The builders of the substrate can make the future described above *possible*. They cannot make it *happen*. Possibility is the technical contribution; adoption is a human question, and human questions do not resolve on the timelines that technical ones do.

---

## A note on agency

These futures are available. The substrate to build them toward exists in early form now and is being actively extended. Whether any given sector adopts it, whether the Stewards that emerge serve their principals honestly, whether the reputation systems resist capture, whether the economic incentives align with the epistemic incentives — these are open questions, and nobody building the substrate gets to answer them alone.

What we can say is this: the shape of future these mechanics enable is one where understanding is durable, where attribution is structural, where privacy is relational rather than binary, and where the people doing the work of knowing get to set the terms on which they share it. That shape is a better shape than the one we have now, for most people and most purposes. The work of getting there is worth doing.

If you are reading this and thinking *I would like that future to exist*, the encouraging news is that you are not a spectator. The substrate needs adopters, challengers, builders, skeptics, and the kind of careful ongoing attention that keeps any powerful thing from drifting into its worst version. Every pyramid built, every Steward configured, every claim traced to its evidence, every supersession honestly recorded, every challenge raised and answered in public — all of these are small moves that, accumulated, shift the world toward the futures described here.

The alternative — leaving the substrate to be built by people whose incentives do not match the future you want — is also a choice. It is the default choice, the one that happens if you do nothing. The scenes above describe a world where a lot of people chose differently. That is the only reason any of them exist.
